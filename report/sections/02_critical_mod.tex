\section{Critical Mod}

\subsection{Concept and Critical Aim}

The general idea of \textit{Trial of the Goddess} is simple on the surface.
The player can approach a mysterious statue and pray to it.
Instead of remaining a decorative object, the statue answers and offers a trial.
The player is asked to travel \textit{Beyond the Veil}, retrieve the \textit{Sacred Flower},
and return it to the Goddess. To make this possible, the player receives
the \textit{Blade of Balance}, a weapon that seems like an overwhelming gift at first.
It can defeat enemies almost instantly, and the quest appears straightforward:
accept the blade, find the flower, survive, and come back.

However, the mod is built around the idea that this simplicity is deceptive.
The trial is much harder than it first appears because every gift from the Goddess also has a cost.
The Blade of Balance makes the player extremely powerful, but it also makes them extremely vulnerable.
While the blade is equipped, the player is reduced to one hit point, meaning that even the
weakest enemy can become fatal. The quest is therefore not only about finding an object, but about
learning how to move through danger while carrying a power that can easily become a liability.

The first critical aim of the mod is to change the meaning of an existing object in Hytale.
The statues in the game already feel important: they look ancient, sacred, and connected to a
larger mythology. However, when they remain mostly decorative, their meaning stays atmospheric
rather than mechanical. My mod uses this gap as a starting point. By making one of these statues
respond to the player, speak through title overlays, offer a trial, and permanently change
the player's state, the statue becomes more than background worldbuilding. It becomes a threshold
between ordinary exploration and a ritualized form of danger.

This changes how the player reads the environment. A statue is no longer just something to
look at or walk past. It becomes something the player approaches with uncertainty:
it might offer power, but that power has consequences. In this way, the mod gives the player a
hint of hidden lore without fully explaining it. The Goddess is not presented as a normal quest
giver or NPC. She is a fading presence attached to stone, memory, and ritual.
This makes the interaction feel less like receiving a standard objective and more like
disturbing something old that still has agency.

The second critical aim is to change how the player understands combat power.
In a normal combat situation, stronger weapons usually make the player feel safer.
If the player can block, absorb damage, and slowly defeat enemies, then combat often
becomes a question of damage output and equipment strength. Large monsters appear
threatening because they look powerful, have more health, and can deal more damage.
Small enemies, such as rats, usually feel like minor obstacles.
They are annoying, but not frightening.

The Blade of Balance reverses this expectation.
It makes the player almost absurdly powerful, but only under very unstable conditions.
Since the blade can defeat enemies in one hit, a
giant monster is no longer necessarily the greatest threat.
Its large body and slower movement can even make it easier to hit.
By contrast, a group of small enemies becomes much more dangerous.
A herd of rats is fast, difficult to control, and hard to eliminate
cleanly when the player can only survive one mistake.
The player's fear shifts from the impressive enemy to the inconvenient one.

This shift becomes stronger through the blade's recharge mechanic.
The Blade of Balance can only deliver three empowered hits before it needs
to recharge for five seconds. On paper, five seconds does not sound long.
In practice, it changes the whole rhythm of combat.
If the blade runs out of power while the player is being chased by
several small enemies, those five seconds become a long period of panic.
The player cannot simply keep attacking.
They must dodge, retreat, hide, reposition, or avoid the fight altogether.
The mod therefore turns combat into a problem of timing and movement rather than only damage.

The result is a form of power that does not create comfort.
The player is given a weapon that looks like a reward, but behaves like a curse.
It asks the player to think differently about strength:
being able to kill anything does not mean being safe from everything.
This is where the mod becomes critical. It does not just make Hytale harder.
It questions the usual relationship between power, safety, and mastery in combat-heavy games.
The player becomes both hunter and prey at the same time.


\subsection{Gameplay Loop}

The gameplay loop begins when the player approaches the Goddess statue
and presses the interaction key F.
Instead of treating the statue as a passive decoration,
the mod turns it into the starting point of the trial.
The player places their hand on the stone,
receives short dialogue lines from the Goddess,
and is then shown a simple choice:
accept the Blade and begin the trial,
or refuse and leave the statue behind.

\subsection{Atmosphere, Lore, and Player Experience}

The gameplay loop is simple,
but the experience of playing it is intentionally tense.
The player receives a weapon that appears divine,
but the interface constantly reminds them that they are close to death.
While holding the Blade of Balance,
the health bar is reduced to one hit point.
The red screen edges,
the low-health warning,
and the constant blinking sound effect
make the player's vulnerability impossible to ignore.
Even before an enemy attacks,
the mod communicates that a single mistake is enough to fail.


This changes how combat feels.
A giant monster looks dangerous,
but its slow movement and large hitbox
make it easier to strike with the Blade.
Small enemies,
especially rats,
become more threatening than expected.
They are fast,
harder to control,
and can surround the player while the blade is recharging.
The five-second recharge is short in technical terms,
but when the player is cornered,
hearing monsters roar nearby,
it feels much longer.
The player is powerful enough to kill almost anything,
but fragile enough to be killed by almost anything.



The atmosphere of the mod is built around contradiction.
At first,
the Goddess statue appears peaceful.
The player approaches it as a sacred object,
not as an enemy.
The Goddess also begins gently,
calling the player
``little wanderer''
and telling them not to be afraid.
Her own sound effect supports this impression:
it is calm,
divine,
and distant,
but also slightly eerie,
as if the voice comes from outside the normal world.

This calmness changes when the player accepts the Blade of Balance.
The sound that plays when receiving the weapon is not peaceful.
It is tense,
eerie,
and almost threatening,
like a reversed gong echoing through the player's ears.
This sound contradicts the Goddess' friendly tone.
The statue looks serene,
and the Goddess speaks softly,
but the weapon she gives the player sounds dangerous.
Because of this,
the player is pushed to question her.
If this is her weapon,
what kind of Goddess is she?
What is the place beyond the veil?
Why is she trapped there?
And why does balance require something so violent?

The Goddess' lore gives this pressure a deeper meaning.
She is not written as a normal quest giver.
She is a fading deity whose name has almost disappeared,
and whose soul is trapped beyond the veil.
The Sacred Flower is not just an item,
but the last piece of herself.
Returning it is therefore not only a quest objective,
but an act of restoration.
The player helps her become whole enough to finally slumber.


The dialogue supports this tone through short,
ritual-like title lines.
Sentences such as
``My soul is trapped beyond the veil''
and
``It is the last piece of myself''
make the statue interaction feel like a prayer
rather than a normal menu.

The first interaction with the statue therefore reads like a short ritual
rather than a standard quest prompt:

Instead,

a world where divine beings can fade,

This creates a contrast between sacred atmosphere and panic.
The Goddess speaks calmly about balance,
loss,
and completion,
while the player is running through combat with one hit point.
The peaceful statue,
the eerie divine voice,
the frightening blade sound,
the monster roars,
and the low-health warning all pull in different directions.
That contradiction is intentional.
The mod uses sound,
interface feedback,
dialogue,
and combat pressure to make the same point:
power is not safety.
The Blade of Balance gives the player divine strength,
but everything around it suggests that this power is unstable,
dangerous,
and not fully trustworthy.

Writing the dialogue was more difficult than expected.
I had a clear concept in mind,
and the Goddess needed enough dialogue to explain the basic story:
who she is,
why the Sacred Flower matters,
and why the player receives the Blade of Balance.
However,
Hytale's title display only worked well with one line of text at a time.
This meant that the dialogue had to be split into many short title pages.
At first,
this made the sequence too long and slow to read.

The challenge was finding a balance between atmosphere and pacing.
The dialogue had to suggest the lore clearly enough
for the player to understand the trial,
but it also had to stay short enough
to avoid becoming boring before the gameplay even started.
After testing the interaction several times,
I noticed that waiting for the full dialogue every time became annoying.
To solve this,
I added a skip feature:
if the player presses the interaction key again while the Goddess is speaking,
the dialogue stops and the offer page opens immediately.
This keeps the ritual feeling for the first interaction,
but makes repeated testing and replaying less frustrating.


From the player's perspective,
the trial should feel like one continuous ritual.
The statue wakes,
the Goddess speaks,
the Blade demands a price,
the Sacred Flower appears,
and the trial either collapses through death
or closes through completion.
In the code,
however,
this ritual is not one object or one script.
It is assembled from many smaller systems that have to pass control to each other
at the right moment.

\subsection{Implementation Overview}

At first glance,
\textit{Trial of the Goddess} looks like a small mod:
a statue is used,
a blade is granted,
a flower is retrieved,
and the ritual is completed.
In the implementation,
this simple loop becomes a network of systems:
state management,
dialogue timing,
custom UI,
damage handling,
monster spawning,
block interaction,
objective tracking,
asset configuration,
persistence,
and cleanup logic.

\subsubsection{Asset Pack and JSON Configuration}

The asset layer was originally planned as a supporting part of the mod.
I expected the main logic to happen in Java,
while assets would only provide the statue,
the Blade of Balance,
the Sacred Flower,
and the sound effects.
This turned out to be too simple.
In Hytale,
not every block automatically creates an interaction event.
The Goddess statue only became usable after its JSON configuration was changed
to point to the custom \texttt{GoddessTrial\_Statue} interaction.

This made asset files part of the actual implementation logic.
To find the right structure,
I had to inspect existing Hytale JSON files
and compare the statue to other interactive blocks.
The same happened with the Sacred Flower.
At first,
the plugin only remembered the position of an ordinary flower
and treated that position as special.
The final version uses a custom Sacred Flower asset with its own item/block ID,
quest tags,
a purple texture,
and a light property that makes it glow.
The objective is therefore visible in the world,
not only hidden in Java state.

One unresolved asset issue remains:
the Sacred Flower appears correctly in the world,
but the inventory displays its internal item ID
instead of the name from the language file.
I could not determine whether this was caused by the language file path,
the translation key,
or Hytale's current asset loading behavior.
Since it does not affect the trial logic,
I left it as a minor visual bug.

The asset layer therefore does more than provide visuals.
It defines which objects can be interacted with,
which items and sounds exist,
where the flower can spawn,
and how the player recognizes the objective.


\subsubsection{Entry Points}

The mod has two entry points:
the Goddess statue and the \texttt{/trial} commands.
The commands were mainly useful during development,
because they allowed me to test starting,
accepting,
refusing,
resetting,
and checking the trial before the statue interaction worked reliably.
In the final mod,
the statue is the main player-facing entry point,
while the commands remain as debugging and recovery tools.

The main challenge was that the same statue interaction had to mean different things
depending on the current situation.
It can start the prayer sequence,
skip already-running dialogue,
remind the player to find the flower,
complete the ritual,
or remain silent after the Goddess is gone.
The statue therefore became a state-dependent gateway rather than a simple button.

This also required persistent storage.
A completed trial must survive disconnects and restarts,
otherwise the Goddess could offer the ritual again.
Unfinished trials,
however,
are not restored after a restart.
This was a deliberate simplification:
restoring active trials created unstable cases with old items,
leftover flowers,
monsters,
and health effects.
Instead,
unfinished trials are treated as failed and cleaned up when the player returns.


\subsubsection{Trial Core}

The Trial Core is the rule system behind the ritual.
It decides which actions are valid at which moment,
while other systems create the visible effects.
The trial behaves like a small state machine:
the player moves from no trial,
to an offered trial,
to an active trial,
and finally either back to no trial after failure
or into the permanent completed state.

This shared state prevents different parts of the mod from contradicting each other.
The statue,
commands,
UI buttons,
death handling,
flower pickup,
and cleanup systems all depend on the same trial phase.
Without this shared source of truth,
the player could accept a trial that was never offered,
complete a trial that is not active,
or restart the ritual after it has already been completed.

\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[height=0.65\textheight,keepaspectratio]{images/statuelogic}

    \small
    Figure: State-dependent behavior of the Goddess statue.
    The same interaction can start the prayer sequence,
    skip dialogue,
    remind the player,
    complete the ritual,
    or stay silent depending on the current trial state.
\end{center}

The most important design choice is that \texttt{COMPLETED} is permanent.
Once the Goddess has received the Sacred Flower,
the trial cannot be offered again.
This makes the state machine part of the story:
failure resets the ritual,
but success changes the world for that player.
The Goddess is gone,
the statue no longer offers the trial,
and the Blade remains as proof that the ritual happened.


\subsubsection{Gameplay Systems}

The gameplay systems turn an accepted trial into a playable challenge.
After the Trial Core validates the transition into the active phase,
the mod removes leftover trial items,
clears old flower positions,
spawns the Sacred Flower,
grants the Blade of Balance,
reduces the player's health,
and spawns the monster wave.

The Blade of Balance is the central gameplay system.
It was designed around a contradiction:
the player should become extremely powerful,
but also extremely vulnerable.
The final version does not reduce maximum health.
Instead,
while the Blade is equipped,
current health is repeatedly forced back to one hit point.
If the player heals while holding it,
the system pushes the health down again.

\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[height=0.65\textheight,keepaspectratio]{images/swordlogic}

    \small
    Figure: Logic of the Blade of Balance.
    While the blade is held during or after the trial,
    the player is kept at 1 HP.
    Its empowered attacks can only be used a limited number of times
    before a recharge phase begins.
\end{center}

The damage system adds a second limitation.
A permanently infinite-damage weapon removed too much tension,
so the Blade has three empowered hits before it must recharge for five seconds.
This creates short windows of power followed by forced weakness.
The player can kill almost anything,
but cannot attack endlessly.

Monster spawning supports this design.
The initial wave contains flying,
small,
normal,
and giant monsters,
plus a concentrated herd of rats.
The rat herd became important because small enemies are harder to control
when the player has only one hit point
and the Blade may be recharging.
A planned reinforcement system was disabled,
because spawning NPCs from inside a ticking system conflicted with Hytale's
entity store processing.
The initial wave remains,
but automatic reinforcements were removed for stability.

The Sacred Flower objective also had to be simplified.
Random terrain-based spawning was unreliable:
the flower could appear in the air,
underground,
inside grass,
too far away,
or at the wrong height.
The final version uses manually configured flower positions on a fixed map.
The positions are shuffled when the trial starts,
and the spawner tries them until a valid one works.


\subsubsection{Player Feedback}

The feedback layer makes the trial understandable and atmospheric.
Without dialogue,
UI,
objectives,
chat messages,
and sound effects,
the mod would still work mechanically,
but it would feel like a debug script:
a sword appears,
health changes,
monsters spawn,
and a flower has to be found.

The Goddess dialogue was difficult because Hytale's title display worked best
with one short line at a time.
My first version was too long and slowed the beginning of the trial down.
I rewrote it into shorter,
ritual-like title pages
and added a skip feature:
pressing the interaction key again while the Goddess is speaking
opens the offer UI immediately.
This keeps the atmosphere for the first interaction,
but makes repeated testing and replaying less frustrating.

Sound became part of the same feedback system.
Custom sound events were added for the Goddess voice
and for receiving the Blade of Balance.
The Goddess sound is calm and divine,
while the Blade sound is tense and eerie.
This contrast supports the uncertainty around the Goddess:
her words are gentle,
but her weapon feels dangerous.

The objective system was simplified for reliability.
I originally wanted it to update after the Sacred Flower was picked up,
but mid-trial objective updates did not behave reliably.
The final objective therefore gives the full instruction from the beginning:
find the Sacred Flower nearby and return it to the statue.
A compass marker was also planned,
but abandoned because it did not appear reliably.
Instead,
the flower itself became the guide through its purple glow.


\subsubsection{Cleanup Systems}

Cleanup became one of the most important parts of the plugin
because the trial temporarily changes the player,
the inventory,
and the world.
It gives special items,
places a flower block,
spawns monsters,
changes health behavior,
tracks objectives,
and stores trial state.
If any of these remain after death,
disconnect,
restart,
or failed testing,
the world quickly becomes polluted with leftover objects and broken states.

The final cleanup behavior is defensive.
On death,
the trial is failed,
the objective is cleared,
temporary items are removed,
Blade energy is cleared,
flower cleanup is attempted,
and monster cleanup is scheduled.
On join,
a delayed validation checks whether cleanup is pending
and removes leftover state where possible.
On server shutdown,
ongoing trials are marked as failed
so that cleanup can continue after restart.
For debugging,
\texttt{/trial reload} performs a stronger manual reset
by clearing trial items,
objectives,
flower positions,
health behavior,
and trial state.

Cleanup also separates failure from success.
If the player dies,
the trial resets and can be attempted again.
If the player completes the ritual,
the Goddess becomes whole,
the completed state is saved permanently,
and the player keeps the Blade of Balance as a reward.
In this way,
cleanup is not only technical maintenance.
It protects the story state of the mod.


\subsection{Working Inside Hytale's Development Pipeline}
\label{subsec:hytale-pipeline-limits}

After explaining the architecture of the mod,
it becomes clearer how strongly the final design was shaped by Hytale's official
development pipeline.
The Hytale Development Toolkit made the beginning of the project accessible:
it generated the basic plugin structure,
provided a local server configuration,
and made expected tasks relatively straightforward.
Registering commands,
sending chat messages,
opening a custom UI,
giving the player an item,
and changing health values all felt like features the toolkit was designed to support.

At the same time,
this convenience sometimes made the underlying system less transparent.
As someone with a computer science background,
I found it frustrating that many steps were hidden behind generated configurations.
At first,
it was not obvious what the server configuration did,
what it did not do,
and how the plugin,
asset pack,
server,
and client interacted.
Because repeatedly building,
deploying,
stopping,
and restarting the server became inconvenient,
I eventually wrote my own workflow for this instead of relying only on the intended setup.

The clearest example was the Goddess statue.
I first expected the statue interaction to be solvable mainly through Java event listeners.
However,
the statue did not automatically create a useful interaction event.
The important change had to happen in the block's JSON configuration:
the statue needed an interaction section that pointed to my custom
\texttt{GoddessTrial\_Statue} interaction.
This changed how I understood Hytale modding.
The asset files were not just decorative resources;
they were part of the logic that decided what the Java code could react to.

Because of this,
a large part of development consisted of inspecting existing assets.
I repeatedly used PowerShell commands such as
\texttt{Get-ChildItem}
and
\texttt{Get-Content}
to search through Hytale's asset files for examples of swords,
flowers,
furniture,
sound events,
interaction hints,
and block properties.
In practice,
the asset folder became a kind of unofficial documentation.

Several final design choices came from this process.
The first was the Sacred Flower navigation.
I originally wanted to guide the player with a compass or objective marker,
but Hytale's marker systems were difficult to access
and did not behave reliably during testing.
Instead of spending the whole project on an unstable navigation feature,
I changed the design:
the Sacred Flower itself became easier to find
through its purple glow and light effect.
This made the objective less automated,
but more reliable and atmospheric.

The second compromise was flower placement.
I originally wanted the Sacred Flower to spawn through a dynamic terrain search.
This sounded better in theory,
because it would have allowed the trial to work naturally in randomly generated worlds.
In practice,
this proved difficult because the terrain was different every time.
The flower could spawn in the air,
underground,
inside grass,
too far away,
or at invalid terrain positions.
A more complete solution would have required an algorithm that searches the nearby terrain
and generates valid flower spawn candidates automatically.

For this version,
I chose a more controlled solution.
Instead of writing a full terrain-placement system,
I used a fixed world and manually defined a set of possible flower coordinates.
When the trial starts,
these positions are shuffled,
and the spawner tries them until one works.
This solution is not as satisfying as a fully dynamic system,
but it makes the trial playable,
stable,
and easy to document.

The third compromise was monster reinforcement.
The original plan included recurring enemy waves during an active trial.
However,
spawning NPCs from inside a ticking system conflicted with Hytale's
entity store processing.
I therefore kept the initial monster wave,
but disabled automatic reinforcements.
This still creates immediate pressure,
while avoiding an unstable system that could break the trial during play.

These compromises were not simply failures.
They show how the official development pipeline shaped the final mod.
Where Hytale's intended systems worked well,
I used them directly:
custom interactions,
UI pages,
sound events,
item effects,
and block JSON files.
Where they became unreliable,
I changed the design instead of forcing the original plan.
The final mod is therefore not only the result of what I wanted to build,
but also of what the toolkit made practical,
stable,
and testable.


\subsection{Custom Sound and Zelda Inspiration}

The atmosphere of \textit{Trial of the Goddess} is inspired by shrine
and goddess interactions in
\textit{The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild}
and
\textit{Tears of the Kingdom}.
I did not copy these games directly,
but used them as references for the feeling of ancient voices,
ritual interaction,
sacred spaces,
and reward-through-trial.

This also influenced the custom sound effects.
Because the sound design was inspired by existing games,
I did not want to simply copy or reuse copyrighted audio.
Instead,
I recreated the intended feeling myself using the Serum synthesizer.
This was an important part of the modding process,
because creating a mod is not only programming.
It also involves asset creation,
sound design,
and deciding how the final experience should feel.
